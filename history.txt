Of all the subjects which one may attempt to teach,
history might be the most difficult and most important one.
For one, what is considered as "truth" is controversial,
as the following quote attributed to Napoleon Bonaparte:
"History is written by the winners."
And in the version of history written by the winners
they may hide their atrocities.
One reason why teaching history is difficult might be
because the knowledge of such atrocities might be traumatizing
as realized by Alice Hoffman:
"Once you know some things, you can't unknow them.
It's a burden that can never be given away."
Infant humans does not have the capability yet
to carry that burden thus it is usually delayed into
adolescence during which one might have other priorities
as described by G. Stanley Hall:
"Adolescence is when the very worst and best impulses
in the human soul struggle against each other for possession."
Thus those who had the priviledge of not had to struggle for 
survival during their life may not prioritize to study history.
The neglect of such effort might result in an immature adult,
if one does not realize the realization by Virginia Satir:
"The end of adolescence is the beginning of adulthood.
What hasn't been finished then will have to be finished later."
Those who seek to finish what is unfinished might appreciate
the following lesson of history:

First lesson of history might be gratitude.
With the awareness of what one and one's own ancestors
had to go through to survive,
one might feel gratitude towards one's own ancestors.
Second lesson might be respect.
With the awareness of what others and their ancestors
had to go through to survive, one migh realize
what atorcities they might be capable of.
One might then be able to appreciate the wisdom
to treat others with respect to avoid such atrocities.
The third lesson might be peace.
With the awareness of what horrible atrocities one and
the others are capable of, one might then be able to 
appreciate the following words by Ralph Waldo Emerson:
"Peace cannot be achieved through violence,
it can only be attained through understanding."
One might appreciate one of such understanding
by John Spence:
"If you tell the truth, it becomes a part of your past.
If you lie, it becomes a part of your future."
Those who seek the truth might appreciate the
words by Aristotle
"We do not know a truth without knowing its cause."
One might appreciate the explaination of 
one of such such cause by Xun Kuang:
"Human nature is evil,
and goodness is caused by intential activity."
Those who are wondering what such intentional activity might be
might appreciate the advice by Socrates:
"Be kind, for everyone you meet is fighting a hard battle."
Those who seek a way to be kind might appreciate the words 
by Joseph Joubert:
"A part of kindness consists in loving people more than they deserve."
One might appreciate the realization by Jack Kornfield:
"If your compassion does not include yourself, it is incomplete."
Those who wonder how to excersise self compassion might find a hint
in the words by Eckhart Tolle:
"You find peace not by rearranging the circumstances of your life,
but by realizing who you are at the deepest level."
And one might appreciate one of such realization by Ralph Waldo Emerson:
"Nothing can bring you peace but yourself."
To bring peace to oneself one might appreciate the words
by William Makepeace Thackeray:
"Never lose a chance of saying a kind word."
Those who do not know what kind word one may say to oneself
might appreciate the the following mantra:
"We are all on the way to Pro."
