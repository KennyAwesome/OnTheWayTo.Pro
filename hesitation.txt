One might admire Miyamoto Musashi for his quote:
"No Fear, No Hesitation, No Surprise, No Doubt."
One might understand the reason for its admiration
through the quote by John C. Maxwell:
"Successful leaders have the courage to take action
while others hesitate."
One might then wonder what courage is,
to which Winston Churchills words might be insightful:
"Fear is a reaction; Courage is a decision."
One might then wonder how to be more decisive,
to which Guy Kawasakis words give a clue:
"When you give people too many choices
it makes them hesitate and not buy stuff."
To reduce ones choices to be more decisive,
the following idea by Harvey MacKay might be helpful:
"Decide what your priorities are an
 how much time you’ll spend on them.
If you don’t, someone else will."
One might appreciate the Eisenhower Matrix to prioritze:
Priority A: Important and urgent-> Do it now
Priority B: Important but not urgent -> Schedule it
Priority C: Not important but urgent -> Delegate it
Priority D: Not important and not urgent -> Decide not to do it.
Regarding Priority D, one might appreciate the words by Steve Jobs:
"The most important decisions you make are not the things you do,
but the things you decide not to do."
To find the second most important priority might be found
in the word by Bill McDermott:
"What you focus on expands.
Focus on what you are best at and you will be unstoppable."
One might appreciate the advice by Louise Hay:
"Challenges are opportunities for me to grow.
The more I learn,
the more equipped I am to handle whatever situations come up."
Those who do not know what to learn,
might appreciate the words by Alan Cohen:
"Personal growth is not a matter of learning new information
but of unlearning old limits."
One might appreciate the words by Ryan Blair to recognize
what has been the limit:
“If it is important to you, you will find a way.
If not, you’ll find an excuse."
While searching for the way, one might be devasted  by
the realization by Lloyd Dobyns:
"Continual improvement is an unending journey."
One might find courage in the following realization by Albert Camus:
"The struggle itself towards the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart.
One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
One might appreciate the following Swedish proverb:
"Shared joy is double joy,
and shared sorrow is half sorrow."
To share the joy of action and to lighten the sorrow of solitude,
one might appreciate the following mantra:
"We are all on the way to Pro."
