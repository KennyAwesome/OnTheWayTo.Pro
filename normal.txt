Albert Camus realized:
"Nobody realizes that some people
expend tremendous energy merely to be normal."
One might find courage in the words by A.G. Howard:
"Normal is subjective.
Don't ever let anyone tell you you're not normal."
But one might still be left with the question,
why the modern human society oustracizes deviation of individuals,
by declaring them "insane".
This ostracization might be an unconscious safety mechanism against mass insanity,
which Dean Koontz might have wanted to warn:
"But insanity is everywhere these days, and celebrated.
Insanity is rapidly becoming the new normal."
One might appreciate the advice on how to recognize
insanity by Sandy Woodward:
"A total absence of self-doubt is the first sign of insanity."
One might also appreciate another sign to recognize
insanity by Mouloud Benzadi:
"One of the signs of insanity is the belief that
life after death matters more than life on Earth!"
One might appreciate the concise remark by Arthur Schnitzler:
"Martyrdom has always been a proof of the intensity,
never of the correctness of a belief."
Ray Bradbury might that intensity as relativity:
"Insanity is relative.
It depends on who has who locked in what cage."
Once one has realized the cage around oneself
might find oneself in despair.
Those might appreciate the optimismby  Eckhart Tolle:
"To recognize one’s own insanity is,
of course, the arising of sanity,
the beginning of healing and transcendence."
Those who are beginning to heal might appreciate
the following enouragement by George Bernard Shaw:
"The reasonable man adapts himself to the world;
the unreasonable one persists to adapt the world to himself.
Therefore all progress depends on the unreasonable man."
George Bernard Shaw also added:
"Life isn’t about finding yourself.
Life is about creating yourself."
Those who seek to create oneself
might appreciate the words by Henri Bergson:
"To exist is to change,
to change is to mature,
to mature is to go on creating oneself endlessly."
Those who despair again when reading "endlessly"
as descibed by Viktor E. Frankl:
"Pain is only bearable if we know it will end,
not if we deny it exists."
One might appreciate the realization by Viktor E. Frankl
has reached:
"Suffering ceases to be suffering at the moment it finds a meaning."
One might appriciate how Albert Camus found meaning in suffering:
"The struggle itself towards the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart.
One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
Those who cannot imagine might appreciate the encouragement
to be an artist by Charles Horton Cooley:
"An artist cannot fail; it is a success to be one."
Those who wonder when an artist is might appreciate
the realization by Ralph Waldo Emerson:
"Every artist was first an amateur."
Those who do not want to remain an amateur
might then appreciate the understanding by David Halberstam:
"Being a professional means doing your job
on the days you don't want to do it."
Those who are tempted to not do do one's own job with
the excuse of lack of talent might appreciate the
words of encouragement by Derek Jeter:
"There may be people who have more talent than you,
but there's no excuse for anyone to work harder than you do
– and I believe that."
One may appreciate the definition of talent by Arthur Schopenhauer:
"Talent hits a target no one else can hit;
Genius hits a target no one else can see."
One might be tempted to ostracize others with extraordinary talents and goals.
Thus it is required for those in power to excercize compassion.
One approach is to recognise that it is possible to arrive at the same goal,
without specifying the path through teaching as Immanuel Kant said it well:
"Genius is the ability to independently arrive at and
understand concepts that would normally have be taught by another person."
The word "genius" has one origin in latin meaning "creative force",
which is undoubtedly one aspect of all art.
But as every force, that force can reap havoc, if it is left unmastered.
To prevent damage caused hubris of the narcissitic interpretation of "genius",
humilty would be advisable.
To prevent damage caused by naive playfulness of individuals with talents,
humanity is adviced to have enough resources to educate them.
Humanity is advised not to be naive about the containment of those individuals,
as mentioned in the warning by Aristotle:
"There is no great genius without some touch of madness."
Those who are ostrasized as mad might appreciate the
realization by Salvador Dalí:
"There is only one difference between a madman and me.
The madman thinks he is sane. I know I am mad."
Those who struggle to embrace one's own madness
might appreciate the words by Hunter S. Thompson:
"When the going gets weird, the weird turn pro."
Those who seek to turn "pro" might appreciate the
encouraging words by Stephen King:
"Weird love's better than no love at all."
Those who has not been priviledged to experience enough love
to nurture one's own the curiousity might find inspiration
and courage to explore and master oneself by saying:
"We are all on the way to Pro."
