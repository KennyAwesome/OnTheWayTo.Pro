"Information is just bits of data.
Knowledge is putting them together.
Wisdom is transcending them."
After that quote by Ram Dass one might appreciate
the insight by Aristotle about the value of putting
information together :
"The whole is greater than the sum of its parts."
One might then be able to appreciate the bold prophecy
by Naval Ravikant:
"A future society will reunite the smart and the spiritual."
One may encounter the word "transcendence" in the context
called "spirituality" while seeking for wisdom for salvation.
One might then see the wisdom in the the following warning
by an unknown author of the letter to Victor White:
"Be aware of unearned wisdom."
One aspect of the reason to why that author might have wanted to warn,
might revieal in the follwing quote by Isaac Asimov:
"The saddest aspect of life right now is that science gathers
knowledge faster than society gathers wisdom."
As Mark Twain said it well:
"Knowledge becomes wisdom only after it has been put to good use."
One might appreciate the piece of wisdom by Billy Connolly:
"Avoid people who say they know the answer.
Keep the company of people who are trying to understand the question."
The first schools originating in ancient greece inspired to pursue ones curiosity,
but through standartization of tests modern schools became more of a sieve for obedience.
As Sydney Gurewitz Clemens puts it:
"Art has the role in education of helping children become like themselves
instead of more like everyone else." 
Pablo Picasso has already understood the problem:
"All children are artists.
The problem is how to remain an artist one he grows up."
One might see a solution approach to that problem
in the following quote by John Hendrix:
"The ultimate question for a responsible man to ask is not
how he is to extricate himself heroically from the affair,
but how the coming generation is going to live."
One might be worried for the coming generation given
the following knowledge by Oscar Wilde:
"Experience is the hardest kind of teacher.
It gives you the test first and the lesson afterward."
Those migh appreciate the words by Thema Bryant:
"If I had only bad experiences, [...].
If I believed that it is all that exists
then the conclusion would be, I am unworthy. [...]
In order for me to come to a different conclusion that
I am worthy of what I have not yet experienced,
I have to have the belief of the 'more'."
With "more" Thema Bryant was referring to spirituality
definded as:
"It is an awareness of the sacred beyond what we can see."
"Becoming more aware" may be referred as "awakening",
which in Buddism is referred to as "Enlightenment",
requiring an ascetic lifestlye including meditation.
The efficacy of meditation was proven with the progress in science.
One may attribute the birth of scientific methodolgy to Immanuel Kant
who redefined of the word "Enlightenment":
"Enlightenment is man's emergence from his self-incurred immaturity."
According to Kant:
"Maturity is having the courage to use one's own intelligence."
As Stephen Hawking pointed out:
"Intelligence is the ability to adapt to change."
The biggest change one can refuse is to knowledge as Karl Popper states:
"True ignorance is not the absence of knowledge, 
but the refusal to acquire it."
Those who seek to aquire knowledge might appreciate words by Rajneesh:
"Transcendence means going beyond duality.
Attachment means remaining within duality."
One might see a clue for transcendence in
the realization by Rabindranath Tagore:
"Only in love are unity and duality not in conflict."
The word "love" can be understood in many different ways.
Those who are overwhelmed by the diversity of meaning
might appreciate the realization by Oscar Wilde:
"To define is to limit."
As long as everyone remains the freedom of defining
it for oneself it would not end in censorship.
Those who still seek a definition to agree to with consent
might appreciate the definition by Bertolt Brecht:
"Love is the wish to give, not to receive, something."
Those who do not know what to give might appreciate
the words by Rick Warren:
"The most desired gift of love is not diamonds or chocolate.
It is focused attention."
One might appreciate the advice on how to implement love
by Paul Tillich:
"The first duty of love is to listen."
Once one is able to fulfill that first duty of love,
one might be able to find all the "four elements of true love"
described by a Zen Master Thich Nhat Hanh:
1. Meinhu = Brotherhood love (discover the need of others = understanding)
2. Karuna = Compassionate love (try helping their suffering)
3. Mudita = Joyful love (enjoy together, effort to offer joy in times of need)
4. Upeksha = Non discriminatory love (Inclusive and calm love)
After one has gained the ability to recognize the elements of true love,
one might appreciate the warning by Suzanne Wright not to love blindly:
"Love is giving someone the power to completely destroy you,
and hoping that they won’t."
Once one has recognized destructive tendencies,
one might find strength in the words Tupac Shakur
to save oneself:
"You never stop loving someone. You just learn to live without them."
One might appreciate the compassion in the words by Søren Kierkegaard
in such a situation:
"The most painful state of being is remembering the future,
particularly the one you'll never have."
Those who are in that painful state might appreciate
the words by Deepak Chopra:
"In every moment you have the choice to be a victim or a creator."
One might appreciate the encouragement by Vincent Van Gogh,
who chose to be a creator:
"There is nothing more truly artistic than to love people."
One might appreciate his understanding of art:
"Art is to console those who are broken by life."
One might appreciate the understanding of art by Robert Genn:
"Art is a form of love. Art is the ultimate gift. Art heals life."
Those who seek to heal oneself might appreciate
the realization by Yoko Ono:
"Healing yourself is connected with healing others."
In order to heal others and oneself one might appreciate
the theory for an healthy relationship by Robert Sternberg:
1. Passion
2. Intimacy
3. Commitment
There is another modern approach by Matthew Hussey,
which is similar yet different,
describing four stages of a relationship:
1. Admiration (Unrequited love)
2. Mutual attraction (Not only limited to Eros)
3. Commitment (marriage, having/raising children)
4. Compatibility (The unnegotiable things are not a burden to each other.)
One may then realize that there are other forms of love,
as described by ancient greek philosophers distinguishing six forms of love.
The 3 well known distictions of love:
1. Eros = Romantic love
2. Philia = Friendly love
3. Agape = Unconditional love
But there are also lesser known forms of love:
4. Philautia = Self love
5. Xenia = Guest love
6. Storge = Familial love

Erich Fromm realized that such choice might often not be voluntary:
"Infantile love follows the principle: 'I love because I am loved.'
Mature love follows the principe 'I am loved because I love'
Immature love says: 'I love you because I need you.'
Mature love says: I need you because I love you."
One might appreciate the following categorization into four types
of attachment styles observed in infants by John Bowlby
which has been developed further over the years.
There are more fine grained distinction,
but one might appreciate this more simplified version:
1. secure attachment: Stays stable even during tough times.
2. anxious attachment: During tough times seeking more validation.
3. avoidant attachment: During tough times seeking more alone time.
4. anxious and avoidant attachment: Behaves avoidant,
but internally seeking for connection.
